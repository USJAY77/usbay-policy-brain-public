```text
I’m building AI governance infrastructure for enterprises that need enforceable controls, human oversight, and audit-ready evidence around AI decisions.

As AI systems move closer to business-critical workflows, organizations need more than policy documents or post-hoc reporting. They need a way to prove what happened, who approved it, which policy applied, what evidence was checked, and why execution was allowed or blocked.

My work focuses on:

- Human oversight for high-risk AI actions
- Fail-closed enforcement when approval, policy, trust, or provenance evidence is missing
- Audit-ready decision records
- Provenance across AI workflows
- Deterministic validation before execution
- Governance controls that security, compliance, legal, risk, and audit teams can trust

The operating principle is simple:

If an AI action cannot be validated, attributed, and audited, it should not proceed.

I’m especially interested in working with teams moving from AI experimentation into governed deployment. That transition creates hard questions:

- How do we prove a human reviewed this decision?
- How do we block execution when approval evidence is missing?
- How do we preserve provenance across policy, model, workflow, and execution layers?
- How do we show regulators, customers, or internal risk teams that AI controls are actually enforced?
- How do we avoid governance becoming a manual checklist that breaks at scale?

My focus is not AI hype. It is the control layer that makes responsible enterprise adoption possible.

I’m currently speaking with enterprise teams, risk leaders, compliance teams, security leaders, legal teams, and AI operators who need stronger governance around AI workflows, approvals, provenance, and audit evidence.

If your organization is piloting or deploying AI in a regulated, high-risk, or enterprise-critical environment, I’m open to conversations around pilot design, governance gaps, and evidence requirements.
```
